  <section id="overview">

    <h2>Project Overview</h2>

    <p>

      Tumor mutational burden (TMB) is a potential biomarker of tumor immunogenicity, but its prognostic role in colorectal cancer remains unclear because survival is strongly influenced by disease stage and microsatellite instability (MSI). This project evaluates whether TMB is associated with overall survival among colorectal cancer patients in the MSK-CHORD cohort.

    </p>

    <div class="highlight">

      <strong>Main finding:</strong> High TMB appeared associated with better survival in unadjusted Kaplan–Meier analysis, but TMB was not an independent prognostic factor after adjustment for clinical covariates. Interaction and sensitivity analyses suggested stage-dependent and non-linear effects.

    </div>

  </section>


  <section id="data">

    <h2>Data and Cohort Construction</h2>

    <p>

      Data were obtained from the MSK-CHORD dataset through the cBioPortal DataHub. Patient-level and sample-level data were merged by patient ID. The analysis was restricted to colorectal cancer patients, and complete-case analysis was used for variables required in the survival models.

    </p>

    <div class="grid">

      <div class="card"><strong>25,040</strong>merged samples</div>

      <div class="card"><strong>5,543</strong>initial CRC patients</div>

      <div class="card"><strong>5,019</strong>analytic cohort</div>

      <div class="card"><strong>2,000</strong>observed deaths</div>

    </div>

  </section>


  <section id="methods">

    <h2>Statistical Methods</h2>

    <p>

      Kaplan–Meier survival analysis was used to compare unadjusted survival between high- and low-TMB groups. Multivariable Cox proportional hazards models were then fitted to evaluate the adjusted association between log-transformed TMB and overall survival.

    </p>

    <p>

      Stage-stratified and MSI-stratified Cox models were used to account for differences in baseline hazards. Interaction terms were included to assess whether the association between TMB and survival differed by Stage IV status or MSI status. Sensitivity analyses modeled TMB using quartiles and restricted cubic splines to assess potential non-linearity.

    </p>

  </section>


  <section id="results">

    <h2>Results</h2>

    <p>

      In unadjusted Kaplan–Meier analysis, patients with high TMB had better survival compared with those with low TMB. However, after adjustment for stage, MSI status, tumor purity, age, sex, and race, log(TMB) was not significantly associated with overall survival.

    </p>

  <section id="conclusion">

    <h2>Conclusion</h2>

    <p>

      TMB was associated with survival in unadjusted analysis but not after adjustment for clinical covariates. Disease stage and MSI status were stronger predictors of survival. The interaction model suggested that the association between TMB and survival differs by stage, while sensitivity analyses indicated a potentially non-linear relationship.

    </p>

    <p>

      Overall, TMB alone does not appear to be an independent prognostic factor for overall survival in this colorectal cancer cohort, but it may have context-specific relevance when considered alongside stage, MSI status, and treatment context.

    </p>
